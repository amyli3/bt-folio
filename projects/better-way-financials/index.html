Better way financials

Builders can't trust their job financials. The current workflow is fragmented — legacy features, scattered tooling, and unopinionated flows — so builders fall back on manual workarounds, miss early risk signals, and make reactive cash-flow decisions that hurt margins and confidence. This was a shared effort across multiple teams to tighten up financial workflows in Buildertrend so invoice and budget features actually reflect reality.

Role
UI design, UX research, prototyping, usability testing, design strategy, IA
Duration
4 months
Team
Amy Li — Lead designer Kendall Sutton — Project manager Cade Robison & Danny White — Feature leads
Tools
Figma, Figma Make, EnjoyHQ, Qualtrics, Maze, Mixpanel

Goals for the program

  • Establish a clear point of transition between estimating and budgeting phases.
  • Prevent accidental changes to approved contract amounts once a job is underway.
  • Lay the groundwork for guardrails and financial clarity across invoicing features.
  • Ensure jobs start from a solid, locked financial baseline — reducing errors and improving trust across teams and with clients.

Success metric

Have at least 1,000 builders adopt the better-way financial happy-path workflow within a 60-day period, measured across three signals:

  • Proposal usage — approve or decline a proposal at least once.
  • Estimate usage — view the Estimate page ≥ 5 times.
  • Job Costing Budget (JCB) usage — view the JCB page ≥ 5 times.
Part 1 · Foundation first

Set contract price — establish financial trust

The problem. Flexible, manual edits to contract price were producing inconsistent, error-prone data. Proposals, legacy budgets, and other financial workflows didn't reliably sync with the Job Costing Budget, so the job costing budget and job price summary often disagreed — eroding builder trust.

The goal. Prevent accidental changes to approved contract amounts once a job is underway, and give builders a single reliable moment where the contract price is locked and everything downstream can trust it.

Meet the users

We mapped three builder archetypes whose workflows touch contract price differently: fixed-price builders who sign a contract up front and need that number to stop moving; cost-plus / open-book builders whose "contract price" evolves with actuals; and remodelers, whose scope shifts mid-job more than any other segment. The current workflow treats them all the same — and fails each of them in different ways.

Where the current workflow broke

  • Job details modal — contract price could be edited at any time with no signal that downstream features were relying on that number.
  • Estimate header — summary totals didn't match what builders saw on the JCB or JPS, so nothing felt authoritative.
  • Open-book jobs — builders wanted to compare contract price against running costs, but the two numbers lived in different mental models without a clean bridge.

Research method

Two rounds of customer interviews — one with fixed-price builders, one with open-book — plus workshop & stakeholder syncs to align with PMs and engineers affected by downstream changes. Goal: separate what builders needed the system to enforce from what was just a preference.

The fixed-price interview gave us the clearest mandate — if the contract price can move without a gate, no number built on top of it can be trusted.

Design iterations

Iteration 1 — Contract price & remaining balance

Introduced an explicit "contract price set" state that gated downstream edits. Once set, changes required a new pathway (a change order or a reset) rather than silent edits. Remaining balance finally had a stable baseline to subtract from.

Iteration 2A — Selections & allowances

Surface selections and allowances in a clear before/after so clients can see what their budgeted allowance was for a selection choice, what they actually picked, and how it impacts total contract price. This was the biggest consistency gap between the JCB and the JPS.

Iteration 2B — Print page

Align the print page one-to-one with what builders see on-screen. Clients were getting PDFs that looked different from the live view — fuel for trust issues even when the numbers were right.

Iteration 3 — Client portal

Clients see the same JPS structure the builder sees — no secret internal view — but scoped to the language and fields relevant to them. "What I owe, what I've paid, what's coming."

Iteration 4 — What you see is what you get

Enforce visual parity across every surface (JCB, JPS, print, client portal). If a number changes in one place, it changes in all of them — and if it can't, we surface the reason explicitly.

Impact from Part 1

  • JPS became a source of truth for both cost-plus and fixed-price jobs — eliminating many legacy reconciliation workarounds.
  • By locking contract price and surfacing allowances, COs, payments, and selections at the JPS, we reduced builder confusion features because locks (change logs, locking) exposed the why behind downstream numbers.
  • Trust — the qualitative win — showed up immediately in follow-up interviews.
Part 2 · Building trust

Improve financial visibility — Job price summary

The problem. The Job Price Summary failed to present an accurate view of contract price and remaining client balance — especially for cost-plus jobs. It pulled fragmented legacy data, didn't reflect the newly locked contract-price workflow, and carried inaccurate selection/allowance numbers. Builders and homeowners couldn't rely on it for billing or forecasting.

"The job price summary…doesn't align with a cost-plus contract…there's an immediate disconnect…we're talking tens of thousands of dollars." — Sam Roy & Josh Fulbright, Tongue & Groove Property Services

What was broken, specifically

Annotating the legacy JPS against real job data, the list of issues clustered into six patterns:

Legacy JPS modal, annotated during audit — five of the six recurring issue patterns visible on a single screen.

Legacy JPS print page — the print view had drifted from the modal, so builders sent PDFs that disagreed with the on-screen numbers.

  • Contract price needs to be set — the page assumed a value that didn't exist in cost-plus flows.
  • Selections numbers aren't accurate — pulled from a stale source that didn't match the JCB.
  • Need client-facing language — copy leaked internal jargon to homeowners.
  • Data we no longer use — legacy fields that nobody could explain.
  • Inaccurate due to data changes — downstream values didn't re-render when source data moved.
  • UI layout updates needed to match modal updates — the page hadn't been refreshed alongside the rest of the financial workflow.

The goal. Give builders an accurate and shareable view of how much their client owes and what's left to pay — scoped to whatever contract type the job is on.

Key design decisions

  • Contract type drives the layout. Fixed-price and cost-plus now render with different headline numbers — remaining balance vs. running total vs. cap — instead of pretending both can fit the same template.
  • Selections and allowances roll up cleanly. The JPS now mirrors the JCB for allowances and selections so the two pages agree to the penny.
  • Client portal parity. The homeowner sees the same structure the builder sees, stripped to the fields relevant to them — no "internal" version of the truth.
Part 3 · Building trust

Improve financial visibility — Job costing budget

The problem. The JCB's projected cost and job-level gross profit were inaccurate and opaque. GCs couldn't reliably judge job financial health, so they kept reaching back for the legacy budget — blocking the business goal of migrating users to the JCB and retiring the legacy budget.

The goal. Restore confidence in the JCB by making projected cost and gross profit accurate, consistent, and transparent — so GCs adopt the JCB as the canonical job financial view and we can retire the legacy budget.

Why builders still trusted the legacy budget

Before drawing, we audited why builders kept the legacy budget open in a second tab. It wasn't loyalty — it was that the legacy view exposed a mental model the JCB had quietly dropped: projected cost as a single, explainable number with a trail of where each dollar came from.

Experimenting with top-level insight visuals

We prototyped several ways of surfacing job health — progress bars vs. variance deltas vs. traffic-light strips — and tested them with fixed-price and open-book builders separately. The two segments read the same visual very differently:

  • Fixed-price builders wanted headline variance against the contract price; they treated "% complete" as noise.
  • Open-book builders wanted running totals with a confidence band; the contract price wasn't the interesting number — actuals vs. budget was.

So projected cost became configurable at the job level, with defaults driven by contract type. Same data, two reads.

What the design locks in

  • One projected-cost formula per job, with its inputs visible inline rather than buried in a definition.
  • Gross profit shown with its derivation — not just a number, but how it was built from contract price, costs, and approved COs.
  • Legacy-budget parity checks ship alongside the feature so GCs can verify their old view against the new one during the migration window.

Reflection

Better Way Financials wasn't one feature — it was three surfaces (contract price, JPS, JCB) that had drifted apart over years of independent releases. The hardest part wasn't designing any single screen; it was getting everyone aligned on a single number flowing through all three, and on which team owned what when it moved. The pattern that worked: lock a source of truth (contract price), then work outward surface by surface making sure each one reads from it correctly.

If I did it again I'd invest earlier in the "what does this number mean?" inline documentation. A lot of the trust issues weren't about wrong numbers — they were about numbers whose definition had quietly changed between releases.
