Progress Invoicing

Building the progress invoice workflow that builders actually adopt mid-job — removing switching costs, bringing in the financial data they need, and making Buildertrend the place where pay applications happen instead of the input to Excel.

Role
Design, prototyping, research, problem solving
Timeline
Nov 2025 — ongoing
Team
Kendall Sutton (PM) · Cade Robison (Lead Developer) · Amy Li (Design)

The problem

"The core challenge is not segment fit or discoverability, but switching costs and trust. Builders are particularly reluctant to change invoicing and accounting practices mid-job." — Progress Invoice Adoption thesis, Ship of Theseus

The feature exists. Buildertrend has progress invoicing. The real problem is adoption: builders who are already using the platform still pull their data out into Excel every month because the existing workflow doesn't meet them where they are. They don't trust it enough to move a live job onto it.

"Builders are currently taking information from disperse locations around Buildertrend to develop custom solutions in external platforms to create the progress invoice… time intensive… multiple opportunities for errors." — Progress Invoice Positioning

And a structural blocker compounds it: progress invoicing "cannot be adopted on active jobs because historical invoice data does not flow into progress invoices." A builder in the middle of a project has no clean path to switch — which is most of the builders we most want to convert.

What we've shipped

This has been a steady release-by-release rebuild, tightening the workflow one seam at a time.

  • Continuation sheet (G703-style SOV) populated from the estimate's client price, with Excel export preserving formulas for Completed & Stored to Date, % Complete, and Balance Remaining. Limited release November 2025 to a ~10-account CAB.
  • Invoicing by dollar amount — not just by percentage — GA March 31, 2026.
  • Invoice Activity Log — GA April 13, 2026.
  • Change orders on progress invoices — approved COs appear as grouped rows directly in the SOV so builders bill against them without re-keying.
  • Grouping strategy toggle (cost code vs. estimate group) — the same invoice viewed the way the builder thinks about the job.
  • Costs on progress invoices (Open Book) — bills, time-clock entries, accounting costs flow in with markup. Cost floors prevent under-invoicing.
  • Mobile responsiveness across invoice, deposits, and credit-memo flows.

What's next

Q2 2026 roadmap, in order of release:

  • Open Book progress invoicing — Release 4/24
  • R1.6 — Adding costs to progress invoices — In development
  • R1.7 — Selection invoicing on progress invoices
  • R1.8 — Online payments for progress invoices — In discovery

Running alongside: a discovery track on progress invoices for in-flight jobs — the largest adoption blocker by the team's own framing, and the piece most directly tied to the "switching costs" thesis.

Design approach

Because the problem is adoption rather than feature coverage, design decisions ladder up to a single question: will a builder three months into a job trust this enough to move their pay application into Buildertrend?

The prototype below is where I've been working through how that trust gets built — detail by detail:

  • Column visibility that responds to the job's reality — Revised budget and Approved changes surface only when change orders exist, so the default flow stays quiet and legible.
  • Two grouping views (cost code vs. estimate group) on the same invoice, because the builder's mental model and the bank's expected layout aren't always the same.
  • Inline reconciliation for approved change orders and selections — avoid the re-entry errors that push builders back into Excel.
  • A client-facing view that shows upcoming billings, not just paid ones — closing the visibility gap homeowners flagged in site visits.

How we're measuring it

"15 CO+ builders agree that client financials are no longer an adoption blocker." — Ship of Theseus Q2 2026 goal

This is deliberately an outcome metric, not an output one. Not "features shipped" — "builders told us this is enough." Leading indicators we're watching: percentage of builders creating multiple progress invoices per job, percentage released, adoption on in-flight jobs, and time from first invoice creation to first release.

That framing shapes every design decision. The goal isn't parity with Excel or Procore. It's trust that survives contact with a real job, at a real builder, on a real Monday.
