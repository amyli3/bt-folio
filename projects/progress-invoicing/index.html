Progress Invoicing

Bringing AIA-compliant billing into Buildertrend so commercial and high-end residential builders can send pay applications without ever leaving the platform.

Role
Design & prototyping
Timeline

Team

The problem

"Despite progress invoicing capabilities, builders cannot reliably produce invoicing documentation that meets funding-oversight party's (bank, architect, client) compliance requirements directly from the platform, forcing them to recreate or adjust pay applications outside the system." — AIA Billing / Progress Invoicing Dev Kickoff, April 2026

For upmarket builders — commercial contractors and high-end custom home builders — AIA-style billing isn't a nice-to-have. It's the contractual format their banks, architects, and clients require. When Buildertrend can't produce a compliant pay application, two things happen: existing customers drop invoicing out of the platform into Excel, and prospective customers walk away to Procore.

The current progress invoicing build gets us roughly 70% of the way there — the foundation of a Schedule of Values, percent-complete billing, and change-order handling is solid. The missing 30% is the documentation layer: the cover sheet (G702), SOV continuation (G703), retainage, stored materials, signatures, and lien waivers.

Discovery

Research started in March 2026 with a round of builder interviews — Shiya Markovic at Royal Builders, Kira Wohlefeil at Onesta GC, Mary Stokes at Williams Construction — alongside conversations with referred leads (Ross Built, Hybar, MFP, CMC) and a site visit to Better Place Design & Build.

A few signals surfaced consistently:

  • AIA is the blocker, not the bonus. Multiple upmarket prospects told us AIA billing decided the deal. MFP had left Buildertrend for Procore explicitly over this gap.
  • Builders are already doing the work — just not in BT. Teams recreate pay applications in Excel every month, then manually reconcile numbers back. The data exists; the output doesn't.
  • Clients want visibility too. The site visit at Better Place surfaced an adjacent pain: the client portal only shows paid invoices, hiding upcoming progress billings. Clients want full visibility.

What builders need from an AIA workflow

Must solve

  • Cover sheet (G702) generation
  • SOV customization & continuation sheet (G703)
  • Retainage input & tracking
  • Stored materials
  • Cover-sheet signatures
  • Lien waivers — collect & generate
  • Sending pay apps to external stakeholders

De-prioritized for v1

  • Advanced audit trails
  • Suggestive % complete
  • Deep workflow automation

Design approach

The prototype below is built around the AIA G702 / G703 mental model — the form most of these builders already know by heart. Rather than invent a new layout, we made the grid feel familiar while pulling data from the live Schedule of Values, so numbers update themselves when a change order is approved or a cost code is billed against.

Interaction decisions I've been prototyping through:

  • Toggling Scheduled value → Revised budget columns on when change orders exist, hiding them by default to keep the cleaner workflow legible.
  • Grouping the SOV by cost code vs. by phase, so the same invoice can be viewed the way the builder thinks about it and the way the bank expects to see it.
  • Inline selections and change-order reconciliation — pulling approved COs into the billing period without re-entering amounts.
  • A client-facing view so homeowners see what's being billed, not just what's already paid — the Better Place signal, turned into UI.

What's next

The first build milestone is the Cover Sheet — the single document most often demanded by banks and architects and the fastest unlock for existing customers already doing progress invoicing in Buildertrend. From there: retainage, stored materials, and the lien-waiver workflow.

The bigger thread this work belongs to is Better Way Financials — restoring trust in the numbers builders see inside Buildertrend, from contract through close-out. Progress invoicing is where that trust becomes tangible: the moment a builder hands a document to someone outside the platform and it holds up.
