Progress Invoicing

Designing progress invoicing that builders actually adopt. Over 7 months, I ran the customer research that shaped the roadmap, prototyped the workflow end-to-end, and made the design calls that moved us from a fragmented legacy feature to a coherent AIA-style billing experience builders will stop exporting out of.

Role
Design, prototyping, research, problem solving
Timeline
Oct 2025 — ongoing
Team
Kendall Sutton (PM) Cade Robison (Lead Developer) Amy Li (Design)

The problem

Builders use progress invoicing as the contractual billing format on commercial and high-end residential jobs — banks, architects, and clients expect AIA G702/G703. When Buildertrend's existing progress invoice couldn't produce that shape cleanly, builders abandoned the workflow and kept their real billing in Excel.

My job as the designer on Ship of Theseus: make the workflow complete enough — and trustworthy enough — that builders don't export out. Every release closes another reason a builder would choose their spreadsheet over Buildertrend.

How many builders still produce pay applications today — a hand-built Excel spreadsheet maintained outside Buildertrend.

Research I ran

Research ran alongside development, not before it. Across Oct 2025 – Apr 2026 I ran a mix of client-presentation interviews, two Maze surveys, a structured card sort, and follow-up builder interviews — each one tied to a specific decision we were about to make.

Client-presentation interviews (Sept – Oct 2025)

The earliest signals came from sessions with Kim Handler, Joan Tyer, and Lance Lee, where I walked builders through early AIA wireframes. Two reactions locked in design directions we'd return to for months:

"We'd be able to have a 'blue bar' for each change order and no longer have to add the 'CO #XXX' to every line item… LOVE IT!" — Kim Handler, client-presentation interview
"That actually does a lot of what I was doing in my spreadsheet. I like it." — Joan Tyer, client-presentation interview

Kim's quote set the direction for how we'd show change orders (grouped rows, not inline columns) before we ever went to Maze. Joan's was the first concrete evidence that the "Excel abandonment" thesis was real — not an assumption.

Maze card sort (Feb 2026)

I built and launched a Maze card sort with our CAB to rank 9 progress-invoice features as must-have vs. nice-to-have. The results reshaped the release plan — a few findings were the opposite of what we'd assumed going in.

What the CAB ranked

Must solve

  • Client preview of invoices
  • In-progress jobs support
  • $ amount entry (not just %)
  • Client send notifications
  • Buildertrend Payments
  • Retainage (8 / 14 builders)

Nice to have

  • Draw package — PDF + attachments ordered by cost code (7 / 14)
  • Cover sheet (G702)
  • Auto-generated change orders for overages
  • Lien waivers (5 / 14) — dropped from must-have

Maze survey on change-order layout (Dec 2025)

Before committing to the grouped-row pattern, I built a focused Maze survey with 9 CAB builders to validate it against the alternative (extra columns per line item). Six of nine preferred grouped rows; five of nine prioritized persisting their estimate-group layout over any other layout preference. Both findings pointed to the same call.

Builder interviews (ongoing)

Interviews with Billy Anderson, Andrew Couchey, Todd Christensen, Cassie Bayes, Cindy Roth, and John Rainone filled in the quieter details the surveys missed.

"Retainage is hard to deal with in Buildertrend right now… you have to keep a separate spreadsheet just for retainage." — Billy Anderson, Waters Building and Design
"Retainage needs to be auto-calculated… sometimes 10% on labor but 0% on materials." — Andrew Couchey, Riggs Custom Builders
"You bring on new features, but never really show how they should be used in relationship to everything else." — Cindy Roth, Beyond Custom Contracting (on onboarding)

Where my research changed the roadmap

Three moments where what I found pulled the team off a scoped plan and onto a different one.

1. In-progress jobs — the must-have that wasn't

The card sort ranked "enable progress invoicing on in-progress jobs" as a must-have. We'd already scoped work for it. But I ran a follow-up round of interviews with the builders who'd voted for it, and they told me something different: they were fine running their existing AIA outside Buildertrend until a new job started, and they'd happily manually enter $ amounts from previous invoices to catch up. The assumption behind "must-have" was that no workaround existed. It did.

We killed the scope. The engineering capacity redirected to Client Preview customization — the thing the same builders kept mentioning in those same interviews.

2. Scope-reduction pivot (Nov 2025)

I'd built a Lovable prototype I was planning to share with builders in interviews — a high-fidelity walkthrough of the full Open Book progress-invoice flow. Karin flagged a real risk: linking users to an external Lovable URL looked like phishing and could trip email filters. Rather than fight Outlook, I pivoted to an Andrew-taxes-style roadmap-in-UI pattern (showing what's coming inline within Buildertrend). That call unblocked the December 2025 MVP ship.

3. Architecture pivot (Nov 2025)

Andy pushed back on the original design direction — building new invoice infrastructure vs. reusing what we already had. I reworked the workflow designs to match the reuse approach, which turned into the foundation everything has shipped on since. Worth naming because the design isn't just what the screens look like — it's what they're possible to build in the timeline we have.

Design decisions

Throughline on every call: preserve how the builder already thinks about the job, and surface change-order and cost complexity without making the default flow any harder to read.

Change orders as rows, not columns

Validated twice — first by Kim Handler in a Sept 2025 interview ("blue bar for each change order… LOVE IT"), then by the Dec 2025 Maze survey (6 of 9 preferred grouped rows, 5 of 9 prioritized estimate-group persistence). Change orders always appear in their own section regardless of which grouping view is active.

Change Orders for a job — approved COs flow into the progress invoice as grouped rows without re-entry.

Group by cost code or estimate group

Builders think about jobs two ways: the auditor's way (cost code) and their own way (estimate group — framing, foundation, finishes). Both are right for different audiences. The invoice supports both layouts via a toggle that persists per invoice in InvoiceMetadata.LineItemGroupStrategy, with the hierarchy assembled client-side so the switch is instant.

Open Book: costs set a floor, not a cage

When Open Book costs flow in (bills, time-clock, accounting), they raise the floor on each cost code's % complete. A builder can't under-invoice work their team has already done. Cost rows are read-only; the remaining balance is proportionally editable across estimate items. Guardrails, not handcuffs.

Selection overages — the math I worked out

The edge case that took the most design effort: what happens when a selection comes in over its allowance?

  • Same cost code as the allowance: invoice only the overage, and surface it under Approved changes alongside COs — preserves the estimate-group layout, avoids double-count. For multi-item allowances, each overage gets allocated proportionally via (A / T) × O where A is the item amount, T is the total billed in the group, and O is the overage — so a single $500 kitchen overage distributes cleanly across the faucets, countertops, and backsplash that drove it.
  • Different cost code: credit (inverse the allowance) and invoice the full amount under the new cost code. Job totals still reconcile to zero.

Client visibility — the closing gap

The thread we kept hearing from builders: clients want to see what's coming, not just what they've already paid. Builders were releasing invoices early as a hack for this. We're closing that loop with a client-facing financial summary that shows pending invoices, pending selections, and change-order impact before anything gets billed.

Client view of the job price summary — revised client price, remaining balance, pending impact, and selections with allowance overages.

How we're measuring it

"15 CO+ builders agree that client financials are no longer an adoption blocker." — Ship of Theseus Q2 2026 goal

Outcome, not output. Leading indicators I'm watching: percentage of builders creating multiple progress invoices per job, percentage released (not just created), adoption on in-flight jobs via workaround, and time from first invoice creation to first release.

The live risk is recruiting — talking to 15 CO+ builders takes more work than the number suggests — which is why the CAB relationship matters so much and why quick-turn Maze surveys need to stay as sharp as the deep interviews.
